22. More Rectangular Domains: Neumann Problems, Mixed BCs, And Semi-Infinite Strip Problems#

In this lecture we Proceed with the solution of Laplace’s equations on rectangular domains with Neumann, mixed boundary conditions, and on regions which comprise a semi-infinite strip.

Key Concepts

Laplace’s equation; Rectangular domains; The Neumann Problem; Mixed BC and semi-infinite strip problems.

Reference Section: Boyce and Di Prima Section 10.8

22.1. The Neumann Problem On A Rectangle - Only Flux Boundary Conditions#

Example 22.1 (The Neumann Problem)

(22.1)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} u_{xx}+u_{yy} & = & 0, \qquad 0<x<a\quad 0<y<b\\ u_x(0,y) & = & 0\quad u_x(a,y)=f(y)\\ u_y(x,0) & = & 0=u_y(x,b). \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

Let \(u(x,y)=X(x)Y(y)\).

(22.2)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{X^{\prime\prime}(x)}{X(x)}= -\frac{Y^{\prime\prime}(y)}{Y(y)}=\la ^2 \end{eqnarray} \]
(22.3)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} \left.\begin{array}{l}Y^{\prime\prime}(y)+\la^2 Y(y)=0\\ Y^\prime (0)=0=Y^\prime (b)\end{array}\right\}\quad \begin{array}{lcl} Y&=&A\cos\la y+B\sin\la y\\ Y^\prime&=&-A\la\sin\la y+B\la \cos\la y\end{array} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]
(22.4)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} Y^\prime (0) & = & \la B=0\quad \la =0\mbox{ or }B=0.\\ \nonumber\\ Y^\prime (b) & = & -A\la\sin\la b=0\quad\begin{array}{lcl} \la_n &=&(n\pi /b)\quad n=0,1,\ldots\\ Y_n&=&\cos\left(\dst\frac{n\pi y}{b}\right) ,\quad Y_0=1\end{array} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]
(22.5)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} X_n^{\prime\prime} -\la^2 X_n & = & 0\\ X_n^\prime (0) & = & 0 \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

  • \(n=0\): \(X_0^{\prime\prime}=0\)

    \[ X_0=c_0x+D_0\Rightarrow X_0^\prime =c_0\Rightarrow X_0^\prime (0)=c_0=0 \]

  • Choose \(D_0=1\): \(X_0=1\)

    (22.6)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} \begin{array}{rcl} n\geq 1\quad X_n&=&c_n\cosh(\la_n x)+D_n\sinh(\la_n x)\\ X_n^\prime &=&c_n\la \sinh(\la_n x)+D_n\la\cosh(\la_n x)\\ X_n^\prime (0)&=&\la_n D_n=0\end{array} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

  • Choose \(c_n=1\): \(X_n=\cosh(\la_n x)\)

    Thus

    (22.7)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} \left. \begin{array}{lclcl} u_n(x,y)&=&X_nY_n&=&\cosh(\la_n x)\cos (\la_n y)\\ u_0(x,y)&=&X_0Y_0&=&1\end{array}\right\}\mbox{ satisfy homog. BC.\quad} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

    Therefore

    (22.8)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} u(x,y)=A_0+\sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty A_n\cosh\left(\frac{n\pi x}{b}\right)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi y}{b}\right) . \end{eqnarray} \]

    Now \(f(y)=u_x(a,y)\).

    (22.9)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} u_x(x,y) & = & \sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty A_n\left(\frac{n\pi}{b}\right)\sinh \left(\frac{n\pi x}{b}\right)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi y}{b}\right)\\ u_x(a,y) & = & \sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty \left\{ A_n\left(\frac{n\pi}{b}\right) \sinh \left(\frac{n\pi a}{b}\right)\right\} \cos\left(\frac{n\pi y}{b}\right) =f(y)\ldots\phantom{\int\int} \label{eqNeumannExpansion} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

    This is like a Fourier Cosine Series for \(f(y)\) but without the constant term \(a_0\).

    Recall

    (22.10)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} f(y)=\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty a_n\cos\left(\frac{n\pi y}{b}\right) ,\; a_n=\frac{2}{b}\int\limits_0^b f(y)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi y}{b}\right)\, dy. \end{eqnarray} \]

    Thus the expansion (\ref{eqNeumannExpansion}) is consistent only if \(a_0=0\). For this to be true we require that

    (22.11)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} \int\limits_0^b f(y)\, dy=0 \end{eqnarray} \]

if \(\dst\int\limits_0^b f(y)\, dy\ne 0\) then there is no solution to the boundary value problem~1.

Note

  1. If \(\dst\int\limits_0^b f(y)\, dy\ne 0\) there is a {\bf{net flux}} into the domain through the right hand boundary and, since the other boundaries are insulated, there can be no steady solution – the temperature will continually change with time.

  2. If \(\dst\int\limits_0^b f(y)\, dy=0\) there is no net flux through the boundary and a steady state can exist. i.e. It is possible that \(u_{xx}+u_{yy}=u_t=0\). If \(\dst\int\limits_0^b f(y)\, dy=0\) then

    (22.12)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} A_n\left(\frac{n\pi}{b}\right)\sinh\left(\frac{n\pi a}{b}\right) = \frac{2}{b}\int\limits_0^b f(y)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi y}{b}\right)\, dy. \end{eqnarray} \]

    Therefore

    (22.13)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} A_n=\frac{2}{n\pi\sinh\left(\frac{n\pi a}{b}\right)} \int\limits_0^b f(y)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi y}{b}\right)\, dy\quad n\geq 1 \end{eqnarray} \]

    and

    (22.14)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} u_\infty (x,y)=A_0+\sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty A_n\cosh \left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right) \cos\left(\frac{n\pi y}{b}\right) \end{eqnarray} \]

    where \(A_0\) is undetermined. \(u(x,y)\) is said to be known up to an arbitrary constant.

  3. If \(u_\infty (x,y)\) is the steady state of a 2D Heat Equation \(u_t=u_{xx}+u_{yy}\) with \(u(x,y,0)=u_0(x,y)\) then

    (22.15)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} \int\limits_D u_t\, dx\, dy=\int\limits_D \mathbf{\nabla}\cdot \mathbf{\nabla} u\, dx\, dy=\int\limits_{\pa D}\frac{\pa u}{\pa n}\ ds=0. \end{eqnarray} \]

    Therefore

    (22.16)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{\pa}{\pa t}\left(\int\limits_D u\, dx\, dy\right) =0\Rightarrow\int\limits_D u\, dx\, dy=\mbox{ const for all time }=\int\limits_D u_0(x,y)\, dx\, dy.\phantom{\int} \end{eqnarray} \]

    Now

    (22.17)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} \int\limits_{ D} u_\infty (x,y)dx dy =A_0 \times \mbox{ area}(D)=\int\limits_{ D} u_0(x,y)\, dx \end{eqnarray} \]

    Which is the condition that determines \(A_0\).

22.2. Rectangular Domains With Mixed BC#

Example 22.2 (Insulating BC along two sides and specified temperatures on the others)

(22.18)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} \De u & = &u_{xx}+u_{yy}=0\\ 0 & = & u_x(0,y)=u_x(a,y)=u(x,0) \label{eqLaplaceMixed}\\ & & u(x,b)=f(x). \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

Let \(u(x,y)=X(x)Y(y)\).

(22.19)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{X^{\prime\prime}}{X}=-\frac{Y^{\prime\prime}}{Y}=\pm \la^2. \end{eqnarray} \]

Since we have homogeneous BC on \(X^\prime (0)=0=X^\prime (a)\) choose \(-\la^2\).

  1. \(X^{\prime\prime}+\la^2 X=0\quad X^{\prime}(0)=0=X^\prime (a)\).

    (22.20)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray}\begin{array}{lcl} X(x)&=&A\cos\la x+B\sin\la x\\ X^\prime (0)&=&B\la =0\Rightarrow B=0\end{array}\begin{array}{lcl} X^\prime (x)&=&-A\la \sin (\la x)+B\la \cos (\la x)\\ X^\prime (a)&=&-A\la\sin (\la a)=0\end{array} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

    Therefore

    (22.21)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} \la_n =(n\pi /a)\quad n=0,1,2,\ldots\quad X_n(x)=\cos\left(\frac{n\pi y}{a}\right) \end{eqnarray} \]

    are eigenfunctions and eigenvalues.

  2. \(\la_n \ne 0\): \(Y^{\prime\prime}-\la^2 Y=0\) and \(Y(0)=0\Rightarrow Y_n(y)=A\sinh\dst\left(\frac{n\pi y}{a}\right)\) \(n\ne 0\). Thus

    (22.22)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} u_n(x,y)=\cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right) \sinh\left(\frac{n\pi y}{a}\right) \end{eqnarray} \]

    satisfy homogeneous BC.

\({\la_0 =0}\): In this case the ODE for \(Y_0\) is:

(22.23)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} Y_0^{\prime\prime}&=&0\Rightarrow Y(y)=c_1y+c_2\\ Y_0(0)&=&c_2=0\Rightarrow Y_0(y)=y \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

and \(u_0(x,y)=y\cdot 1\) satisfies the homogeneous BC.

Therefore

(22.24)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} u(x,y) & = & c_0y+\sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty c_n\sinh\left(\frac{n\pi y}{a}\right)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right)\\ u(x,b) & = & \frac{(2c_0b)}{2}+\sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty c_n\sinh\left(\frac{n\pi b}{a}\right)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right) =f(x)\\ (2c_0b) & = & \frac{2}{a}\int\limits_0^a f(x)\, dx;\ \begin{array}{l} c_n\sinh\left(\frac{n\pi b}{a}\right) =\frac{2}{a}\int\limits_0^a f(x)\cos \left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right)\, dx\phantom{\int}\end{array}\\ c_0 & = & \frac{1}{ab}\int\limits_0^a f(x)\, dx;\ \begin{array}{l} c_n=\frac{2}{a\sinh\left(\frac{n\pi b}{a}\right)}\int\limits_0^a f(x)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right)\, dx\end{array}\\ u(x,y) & = & c_0y+\sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty c_n\sinh\left(\frac{n\pi y}{a}\right)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right) . \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

22.3. Semi-Infinite Strip Problems#

Example 22.3 (A Semi-Infinite Strip with Specified Temperatures)

(22.25)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} u_{xx}+u_{yy} & = & 0\qquad 0<x<a,\quad 0<y<\infty\\ u(0,y) & = & 0=u(a,y)\\ u(x,0) & = & f(x)\qquad u(x,y)\ra 0\mbox{ as }y\ra\infty \label{semiHomog} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

Let \(u(x,t)=X(x)T(t)\) and plug into (1a?):

(22.26)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{X^{\prime\prime}(x)}{X(x)}= - \frac{Y^{\prime\prime}(y)}{Y(y)}=-\la^2 \mbox{ since we have homogeneous BC on $X$}. \end{eqnarray} \]
  1. (22.27)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray}\left.\begin{array}{l} X^{\prime\prime}+\la^2 X=0\\ X(0)=0=X(a)\end{array}\right\}\quad\begin{array}{lcl} \la_n &=&n\pi /a\quad n=1,2, \ldots\\ X_n &=&\sin\left(\dst\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right)\end{array} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

  2. \(Y^{\prime\prime} -\la^2 Y=0\quad Y(y)=A\rme^{-\la y}+B\rme^{\la y}\). Since \(u(x,y)\ra 0\) as \(y\ra\infty\) we require \(B=0\). Therefore

    (22.28)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} u_n(x,y)=\rme^{-\la_n y}\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right) \end{eqnarray} \]

    satisfy the homogeneous BC and the BC at \(\infty\). Thus

    (22.29)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} u(x,y)=\sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty c_n\rme^{-\left(\frac{n\pi}{a}\right) y}\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right) . \end{eqnarray} \]
    (22.30)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} f(x)=u(x,0)=\sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty c_n\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right)\Rightarrow c_n=\frac{2}{a}\int\limits_0^a f(x)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right)\, dx.\phantom{\int} \end{eqnarray} \]

Example 22.4 (Semi-Infinite Strip with Inhomogeneous BC)

(22.31)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} u_{xx}+u_{yy} & = & 0\qquad 0<x<a,\quad 0<y<\infty\\ u(0,y) & = & A,\quad B=u(a,y)\\ u(x,0) & = & f(x)\qquad u(x,y)\ra 0\mbox{ as }y\ra\infty \label{semiInHomog} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

Look for a function \(v(x)\) for which \(v^{\prime\prime}=0\) and which satisfies the inhomogeneous BC.

\(v=\al x+\beta\quad v(0)=A=\beta\quad v(a)=\al a+A=B\)

Therefore \(v(x)=\dst\left(\frac{B-A}{a}\right) x+A\).

Now let \(u(x,y)=v(x)+w(x,y)\).

(22.32)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} 0 & = & u_{xx}+u_{yy}=v_{xx}\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\nearrow +w_{xx}+v_{yy}\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\nearrow +w_{yy}\Rightarrow\De w=0\\ A & = & u(0,y)=v(0)+w(0,y)\Rightarrow w(0,y)=0\\ B & = & u(a,y)=v(a)+w(a,y)\Rightarrow w(a,y)=0\\ f(x) & = & u(x,0)=v(x)+w(x,0)\Rightarrow w(x,0)=f(x)-v(x). \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

Thus \(w\) satisfies the same BVP as does \(u\) in Eg.~3 above.

Therefore

(22.33)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} u(x,y)=(B-A)(x/a)+A+\sum\limits_{n=1}^\infty d_n\rme^{-\left(\frac{n\pi}{a}\right) y}\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right) \end{eqnarray} \]

where

(22.34)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} d_n=\frac{2}{a}\int\limits_0^a\left\{ f(x)-v(x)\right\}\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right)\, dx. \end{eqnarray} \]
