13. Wave Equation#

Consider the following initial boundary value problem for the wave equation:

(13.1)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} u_{tt}&=&c^2u_{xx}\quad 0<x<L\\ \mbox{BC:}\quad u(0,t) &=& 0\quad u(L,t)=0\\ \mbox{IC:}\quad u(x,0)&=&f(x)\\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}(x,0)&=&g(x) \label{eqwaveVelIC} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

We introduce a finite difference mesh \(x_n=n\Delta t\), \(t_k=k\Delta t\) and let the corresponding nodal values be denoted by

(13.2)#\[ \begin{eqnarray*} u_n^k\simeq u(x_n,t_k). \end{eqnarray*} \]

Now approximating derivatives by central differences both in space and time we obtain

(13.3)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} \frac{u_n^{k+1}-2u_n^k+u_n^{k-1}}{\Delta t^2}&=&c^2\left(\frac{u_{n+1}^k-2u_n^k+u_{n-1}^k}{\Delta x^2}\right) +O(\Delta x^2,\Delta t^2).\\ \mbox{Therefore}\quad u_n^{k+1}&=&2u_n^k-u_n^{k-1}+{\left(\frac{c\Delta t}{\Delta x}\right)}^2\big( u_{n+1}^k-2u_n^k+u_{n-1}^k\big)\nonumber\\ \underbrace{u_n^{k+1}}_{\mbox{time level $k+1$}}&=& \underbrace{r^2u_{n+1}^k+2(1-r^2)u_n^k+r^2u_{n-1}^k}_{\mbox{time level $k$}}-\underbrace{u_n^{k-1}}_{\mbox{time level $k-1$}} \label{eq_FD_Wavekgen} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

Here \(r=(c\Delta t/\Delta x)\) is known as the Courant Number. We observe that the Discrete Equation (13.3) involves three distinct levels in which known data is transferred from steps \(k-1\) and \(k\) to step \(k+1\).

Initial Conditions - Starting the Solution

The 3-level scheme poses some challenges when imposing the initial conditions. If we imagine a row of false mesh points at time \(t=-\Delta t=t_{-1}\), then the initial velocity condition (13.1) can be approximated using central differences as:

(13.4)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} \frac{u_n^1-u_n^{-1}}{2\Delta t}& = & g(x_n)\label{FalseICWaveEq} \end{eqnarray} \]

therefore

(13.5)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} u_n^{-1}&=&u_n^1-2 \Delta t g(x_n) \label{eqWaveIC} \end{eqnarray} \]

Now we assume that the Discrete Wave Equation (13.3) also holds at \(t=0\) so that

(13.6)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} u_n^1=r^2u_{n+1}^0+2(1-r^2)u_n^0+r^2u_{n-1}^0-u_n^{-1} \label{eq_FD_Wavekeq1} \end{eqnarray} \]

Substituting (13.5) into (13.6) and re-arranging we obtain:

(13.7)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} u_n^1=\frac{1}{2}(r^2u_{n+1}^0+2(1-r^2)u_n^0+r^2u_{n-1}^0)+\Delta t g(x_n) \label{eq_FD_Wavekeq1c} \end{eqnarray} \]

Since \(u_n^0=f(x_n)\) and \(g(x_n)\) are known, we are now in a position to specify the first two rows of nodes. This is sufficient to start the recursion (13.3) for all subsequent steps.

13.1. Stability of the Finite Difference Scheme for the Wave Equation#

Consider the following finite difference approximation to the 1D wave equation:

(13.8)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} u_n^{k+1}=r^2u_{n+1}^k+2(1-r^2)u_n^k+r^2u_{n-1}^k-u_n^{k-1} \label{FDWaveEqGen} \end{eqnarray} \]

and, as in the case of the heat equation, substitute \(\displaystyle u_n^k=\phi_ke^{in\Delta x\theta}\) into (13.8)

(13.9)#\[ \begin{eqnarray*} e^{in\Delta x\theta }\phi _{k+1}=\left( r^{2}e^{i\Delta x\theta }+2\left( 1-r^{2}\right) +r^{2}e^{-i\Delta x\theta }\right) e^{in\Delta x\theta }\phi _{k}-e^{in\Delta x\theta }\phi _{k-1} \end{eqnarray*} \]

Canceling terms and using the double angle formulae

(13.10)#\[\begin{split} \begin{eqnarray} \phi _{k+1} &=&2\left( 1+r^{2}\left( \cos \Delta x\theta -1\right) \right) \phi _{k}-\phi _{k-1} \\ &=&2\left( 1-2r^{2}\sin ^{2}\frac{\Delta x\theta }{2}\right) \phi _{k}-\phi _{k-1} \label{waveStabEq} \end{eqnarray} \end{split}\]

If we now assume that \(\phi _{k}\) has the following exponential form \(\phi _{k}=G^{k}\) then (13.10) reduces to the following quadratic equation:

(13.11)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} G^{2}-2\gamma G+1=0 \label{StabQuadratic} \end{eqnarray} \]

where \(\gamma =\left( 1-2r^{2}\sin ^{2}\frac{\Delta x\theta }{2}\right) \). The solutions of this quadratic equation are given by

(13.12)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} G_{1,2}=\gamma \pm \sqrt{\gamma ^{2}-1} \end{eqnarray} \]

Now since \(G_1\) and \(G_2\) are the roots of this quadratic we may conclude that

(13.13)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} (G-G_{1})(G-G_{2})=G^{2}-(G_{1}+G_{2})G+G_{1}G_{2}=0 \label{StabQuadratic1} \end{eqnarray} \]

Comparing the last terms in these two quadratic equations (\ref{StabQuadratic}) and (13.13) we conclude that

(13.14)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} G_{1}G_{2}=1. \label{unitDiskConstraint} \end{eqnarray} \]

However, for stability of solutions for the form \(\phi _{k}=G^{k}\), we require that \(|G_1|\le 1\) and \(|G_2|\le 1\). Given the constraint (13.14), the only possibility, if the solutions are to be stable, is that \(|G_1|=|G_2|= 1\). Thus \(G\) must fall on the unit disk, which implies that

(13.15)#\[ \begin{eqnarray*} |\gamma|\le 1 \end{eqnarray*} \]

Thus

(13.16)#\[ \begin{eqnarray*} \left| 1-2r^{2}\sin ^{2}\frac{\Delta x\theta }{2}\right|\le 1 \end{eqnarray*} \]

or

(13.17)#\[ \begin{eqnarray*} -1\le 1-2r^{2}\sin ^{2}\frac{\Delta x\theta }{2}\le 1 \end{eqnarray*} \]

so that

(13.18)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} -2\le -2r^{2}\sin ^{2}\frac{\Delta x\theta }{2}\le 0 \label{StabCondWaveEq} \end{eqnarray} \]

The second inequality in (13.18) is satisfied automatically, while the first leads to the condition

(13.19)#\[ \begin{eqnarray*} r^{2}\sin ^{2}\frac{\Delta x\theta }{2}\le 1 \end{eqnarray*} \]

Since the maximum value that \(\sin ^{2}(\frac{\Delta x\theta }{2})\) can achieve is 1, we conclude that the condition for stability is

(13.20)#\[ \begin{eqnarray*} r=(c\Delta t/\Delta x)\le 1 \end{eqnarray*} \]

or

(13.21)#\[ \begin{eqnarray} \Delta t\le \frac{\Delta x }{c} \label{CFL_Wave} \end{eqnarray} \]

The condition (13.21), which imposes an upper bound on the time step that can be used, is known as the Courant-Friedrichs-Lewy or CFL condition.
