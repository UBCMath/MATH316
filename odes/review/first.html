1.1. First Order Equations#

1.1.1. Separable Equations#

A first order differential equation is separable if it is of the form

(1.1)#\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = P(x)Q(y) \]

Solve a separable equation by dividing by \(Q(y)\) and integrating with respect to \(x\)

(1.2)#\[ \int\frac{dy}{Q(y)} = \int P(x) dx \]

This is called the method of separation of variables. For example, find the general solution of the equation

(1.3)#\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = xy \]

Apply the method of separation of variables

(1.4)#\[\begin{split} \int \frac{dy}{y} &= \int x \, dx \\ \ln|y| &= \frac{x}{2} + C \\ y &= A e^{x/2} \end{split}\]

where \(A = e^C \in \mathbb{R}\). We found an explicit closed form solution \(y(x)\) in this case however this is not always possible. For example, find the general solution of the equation

(1.5)#\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{4y}{x(y-3)} \]

by applying the method of separation of variables

(1.6)#\[\begin{split} \int \left( 1 - \frac{3}{y} \right) \, dy &= 4 \int \frac{dx}{x} \\ y - 3 \ln|y| &= 4 \ln|x| + C \\ y &= \ln (x^4 y^3) + C \\ A x^4 y^3 &= e^y \end{split}\]

where \(A = e^C \in \mathbb{R}\). We cannot solve for \(y\) therefore this defines an implicit solution.

1.1.2. Linear Equations#

A first order differential equation is linear if it is of the form

(1.7)#\[ y'(x) + P(x)y = Q(x) \]

Can we find a function \(F(x)\) to multiply (1.7) by in order to turn the left hand side into a derivative of a product

(1.8)#\[ F(x)y' + F(x)P(x)y = F(x)Q(x) \]
(1.9)#\[ (F(x)y)' = F(x)y' + F'(x)y = F(x)Q(x) \]

So let \(F'(x) = F(x)P(x)\) which is a separable equation and so we apply separable of variables

(1.10)#\[\begin{split} \int \frac{dF}{F} &= \int P(x)dx \\ \ln F &= \int P(x)dx \\ F(x) &= e^{\int P(x)dx} \end{split}\]

The function \(F(x)\) is called integrating factor

(1.11)#\[ F(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx} \]

This leads us to an expression for the general solution of (1.7)

(1.12)#\[\begin{split} F(x) y' + F(x)P(x)y &= F(x)Q(x) \\ \left( F(x) y \right)' &= F(x) Q(x) \\ y(x) &= \frac{1}{F(x)} \left( \int F(x)Q(x)dx + C \right) \end{split}\]

For example, letâ€™s apply the integrating factor to find the general solution of the linear equation

(1.13)#\[ y' + 2y = 0 \]

The integrating factor is

(1.14)#\[ F(x) = e^{2x} \]

Therefore we find the solution

(1.15)#\[\begin{split} e^{2x}y' + 2e^{2x}y &= 0 \\ \left( e^{2x}y \right)' &= 0 \\ e^{2x}y &= C \\ y(x) &= C e^{-2x} \end{split}\]

Letâ€™s try another example. Find the unique solution of the equation

(1.16)#\[ \frac{dy}{dx} + \cot(x) y = 5 e^{\cos(x)} \ \ , \ \ \ y(\pi/2) = -4 \]

Identify the functions \(P(x)\) and \(Q(x)\) and find the integrating factor

(1.17)#\[\begin{split} P(x) &= \cot(x) \\ Q(x) &= 5 e^{\cos(x)} \\ F(x) &= e^{\int \cot(x)dx} = e^{\ln(\sin(x))} = \sin(x) \end{split}\]

Compute the general solution

\[\begin{split} \sin(x) y' + \cos(x) y &= 5 e^{\cos(x)}\sin(x) \\ (\sin(x) y)' &= 5 e^{\cos(x)}\sin(x) \\ \sin(x) y &= -5 e^{\cos(x)} + C\\ y(x) &= - \frac{5e^{\cos(x)} - C}{\sin(x)} \\ \end{split}\]

Plug in the initial value to find \(C\)

(1.18)#\[\begin{split} -4 &= y(\pi /2)= - \frac{5 - C}{1} \ \ \Rightarrow \ \ C = 1 \\ \end{split}\]

The unique solution of (1.16) is

(1.19)#\[ y(x) = \frac{1-5 e^{\cos(x)}}{\sin(x)} \]
