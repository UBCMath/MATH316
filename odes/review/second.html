1.2. Second Order Constant Coefficient Equations#

1.2.1. Characteristic Polynomial#

A second order constant coefficient homogeneous equation is of the form

(1.20)#\[ ay'' + by' + cy = 0 \ \ , \ \ \ a,b,c \in \mathbb{R} \]

Introduce notation \(L\) for the linear operator

(1.21)#\[ L[y] = ay'' + by' + cy \]

Let \(y(x) = e^{rx}\) and compute

(1.22)#\[ L \left[ e^{rx} \right] = (ar^2 + br + c) e^{rx} \]

Therefore \(L[y] = 0\) provided \(ar^2 + br + c = 0\) and solutions of (1.20) are determined by roots of the characteristic polynomial

(1.23)#\[ g(r) = ar^2 + br + c = 0 \]

Denote the roots by

(1.24)#\[ r_1 = \frac{- b + \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \ \ \ \text{and} \ \ \ r_2 = \frac{- b - \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \]

and let

(1.25)#\[ \Delta = b^2 - 4ac \]

be the discriminant of the characteristic polynomial.

1.2.2. Case I: Real Distinct Roots#

If \(\Delta > 0\) then \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) are real distinct roots: \(r_1,r_2 \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(r_1 \ne r_2\). The general solution of (1.20) in this case is

(1.26)#\[ y(x)=c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 e^{r_2x} \ \ , \ \ \ c_1 , c_2 \in \mathbb{R} \]

1.2.3. Case II: Real Repeated Roots#

If \(\Delta = 0\) then \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) are real repeated roots: \(r_1,r_2 \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(r_1 = r_2\). In this case, we obtain only one solution

(1.27)#\[ y(x) = e^{r_1 x} \]

How do we get a second solution? Weâ€™ll consider two methods to prove the existence of an another solution.

First Method: Perturbation of the repeated root

Consider a small perturbation \(\epsilon\) to the repeated root (see Fig. 1.1) and the perturbed characteristic polynomial

(1.28)#\[ g(r) = a(r - (r_1 - \epsilon))(r - (r_1 + \epsilon)) = a \left( (r - r_1)^2 - \epsilon^2 \right) \]

In this case, the two very close but distinct roots of \(g(r) = 0\) are given by \(r_1 + \epsilon\) and \(r_1 - \epsilon\) and the general solution of (1.20) is

(1.29)#\[ y(x)= c_1 e^{(r_1 + \epsilon)x} + c_2 e^{(r_1 - \epsilon)x} \ \ , \ \ \ c_1 , c_2 \in \mathbb{R} \]

Choose a special solution by selecting

(1.30)#\[ c_1 = \frac{1}{2\epsilon} = -c_2 \]

and we obtain a family of solutions that depend on the small parameter \(\epsilon\) (see Fig. 1.1)

(1.31)#\[ y(x,\epsilon) = \frac{e^{(r_1 + \epsilon)x} - e^{(r_1 - \epsilon)x}}{2\epsilon} \]

Take the limit as \(\epsilon \rightarrow 0\) using Lâ€™Hospitalâ€™s Rule to find the limiting solution

(1.32)#\[ y(x,\epsilon) = e^{r_1x} \left( \frac{e^{\epsilon x} - e^{-\epsilon x}}{2\epsilon} \right) \longrightarrow x e^{r_1x} \hspace{10mm} \text{as} \ \ \epsilon \rightarrow 0 \]

Therefore we have found that

(1.33)#\[ y(x) = x e^{r_1 x} \]

is another solution of (1.20) in the case \(\Delta = 0\).

Second Method: Take the derivative with respect to \(r\)

Note that the limit (1.32) is equivalent to the derivative

(1.34)#\[ \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} y(x,\epsilon) = \left. \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( e^{rx} \right) \ \right|_{r=r_1} \]

therefore we see that the new solution \(x e^{r_1 x}\) was obtained by taking the derivative of \(y(r,x)= e^{rx}\) with respect to \(r\) and then making the substitution \(r=r_1\). This is, in fact, a general procedure that we will use later in the course. To see why this procedure works, let

(1.35)#\[ y(r,x) = e^{rx} \]

and compute

(1.36)#\[\begin{split} L \left[ y(r,x) \right] &= a(r - r_1)^2 e^{rx} \\ L \left. \left[ \frac{\partial y}{\partial r}(r,x) \right] \right|_{r=r_1} &= \left. \left( 2a(r - r_1) e^{rx} + a(r - r_1)^2 x e^{rx} \right) \right|_{r = r_1} = 0 \end{split}\]

Therefore

(1.37)#\[ \left. \frac{\partial y}{\partial r}(r,x) \right|_{r = r_1} = x e^{r_1 x} \]

is also a solution. Thus, to summarize, the general solution for the case of a repeated root is

(1.38)#\[ y(x) = c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 x e^{r_1 x} \ \ , \ \ \ c_1,c_2 \in \mathbb{R} \]

1.2.4. Case III: Complex Conjugate Roots#

If \(\Delta < 0\) then \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) are complex conjugate roots: \(r_1,r_2 \in \mathbb{C}\) and \(r_1 = \bar{r}_2\). Let

\[ \lambda = -\frac{b}{2a} \hspace{10mm} \text{and} \hspace{10mm} \mu = \frac{\sqrt{4ac-b^2}}{2a} \]

such that

(1.39)#\[ r_1 = \lambda + i \mu \hspace{10mm} \text{and} \hspace{10mm} r_2 = \lambda - i \mu \]

The general solution of (1.20) in this case is

(1.40)#\[\begin{split} y(x) &= c_1 e^{(\lambda + i\mu)x} + c_2 e^{(\lambda - i\mu)x} \\ &= e^{\lambda x} \left( A \cos \mu x + B\sin \mu x \right) \ \ , \ \ \ A,B \in \mathbb{R} \end{split}\]

1.2.5. Examples#

Exmaple 1. Find the general solution of the equation

\[ L[y] = y'' + y' - 6y = 0 \]

The roots of the characteristic polynomial are \(r_1 = 2\) and \(r_2 = -3\) and the general solution is

\[ y(x) = c_1 e^{-3x} + c_2 e^{2x} \ \ , \ \ \ c_1,c_2 \in \mathbb{R} \]

Exmaple 2. Find the general solution of the equation

\[ L[y] = y'' + 6y' + 9y = 0 \]

The roots of the characteristic polynomial are repreated \(r_1 = r_2 = -3\) and the general solution is

\[ y(x) = c_1 e^{-3x} + c_2 x e^{-3x} \ \ , \ \ \ c_1,c_2 \in \mathbb{R} \]

Exmaple 3. Find the general solution of the equation

\[ L[y] = y'' - 4y' + 13y = 0 \]

The roots of the characteristic polynomial are \(r_1 = 2 + 3i\) and \(r_2 = 2 - 3i\) and the general solution is

\[ y(x) = e^{2 x} \left( A \cos 3x + B \sin 3x \right) \]
