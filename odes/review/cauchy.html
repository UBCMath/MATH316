1.3. Cauchy-Euler Equations#

1.3.1. Substitution \(x = e^t\)#

A second order linear equation of the form

(1.41)#\[ L[y] = x^2y'' + \alpha xy' + \beta y = 0 \ \ , \ \ \ \alpha , \beta \in \mathbb{R} \]

is called a Cauchy-Euler equation (or an equidimensional equation). We can solve a Cauchy-Euler equation by using a substitution to transform it into a second order equation with constant coefficients. In particular, let

(1.42)#\[\begin{split} x &= e^t \\ t &= \ln x \end{split}\]

The chain rule gives the relation

(1.43)#\[ \frac{d}{dx} = \frac{d}{dt} \frac{dt}{dx} \ \ \Rightarrow \ \ \frac{d}{dt} = x\frac{d}{dx} \]

and also

(1.44)#\[\begin{split} \frac{d^2}{dt^2} &= x \frac{d}{dx} \left( x \frac{d}{dx} \right) \\ &= x^2 \frac{d^2}{dx^2} + x\frac{d}{dx} \end{split}\]

In particular, we have

(1.45)#\[ x^2 \frac{d^2}{dx^2} = \frac{d^2}{dt^2} - \frac{d}{dt} \]

Therefore, using the substitution (1.42), the equation (1.41) becomes a second order equation with constant coefficients

(1.46)#\[\begin{split} \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} - \frac{dy}{dt} + \alpha \frac{dy}{dt} + \beta y &= 0 \\ \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + (\alpha - 1) \frac{dy}{dt} + \beta y &= 0 \end{split}\]

We know that solutions of (1.46) are of the form \(y = e^{rt}\) therefore to solve (1.41) we use the substitution (1.42) and let

(1.47)#\[\begin{split} y &= x^r \\ y' &= rx^{r-1} \\ y'' &= r(r-1)x^{r-2} \end{split}\]

and (1.41) becomes

(1.48)#\[ \left( r(r-1) + \alpha r + \beta\right) x^r = 0 \]

The characteristic polynomial of the Cauchy-Euler equation (1.41) is

(1.49)#\[ r^2 + (\alpha - 1)r + \beta = 0 \]

with roots

(1.50)#\[\begin{split} r_1 &= \frac{1 - \alpha + \sqrt{(\alpha - 1)^2 - 4\beta}}{2} \\ r_2 &= \frac{1 - \alpha - \sqrt{(\alpha - 1)^2 - 4\beta}}{2} \end{split}\]

We know that the form of the solution of (1.41) depends on the discriminant

(1.51)#\[ \Delta = (\alpha - 1)^2 - 4\beta \]

1.3.2. Case I: Real Distinct Roots#

If \(\Delta > 0\) then the roots \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) are real and distinct and the general solution of (1.41) is

(1.52)#\[ y(x) = c_1x^{r_1} + c_2x^{r_2} \ , \ \ c_1,c_2 \in \mathbb{R} \]

Note that if either \(r_1 < 0\) or \(r_2 < 0\) then \(|y| \to \infty\) as \(x \to 0\).

1.3.3. Case II: Real Repeated Roots#

If \(\Delta = 0\) then the roots are real and repeated and

(1.53)#\[ r_1 = r_2 = \frac{1 - \alpha}{2} \]

We obtain only one solution in this case

(1.54)#\[ y = c_1 x^{r_1} \ , \ \ c_1 \in \mathbb{R} \]

To get a second solution we use the method introduced in Second Order Constant Coefficient Equations (see Case II: Real Repeated Roots) in which we differentiate with respect to the parameter \(r\)

(1.55)#\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( x^r \right) = x^r \ln x \]

Let’s verify that \(y(x) = x^{r_1} \ln x\) is a solution of (1.41). Compute the derivatives with respect to \(x\)

(1.56)#\[\begin{split} (x^r \ln x)' &= rx^{r-1}\ln x + x^{r-1} \\ (x^r \ln x)'' &= r(r-1)x^r\ln x + rx^{r-2} + (r-1)x^{r-2} \end{split}\]

and plug in to (1.41) to find

(1.57)#\[\begin{split} L[x^r \ln x] &= x^2 (x^r \ln x)'' + \alpha x(x^r \ln x)' + \beta (x^r \ln x) \\ &= x^2 \left( r(r-1)x^r\ln x + rx^{r-2} + (r-1)x^{r-2} \right) \\ & \quad \quad + \ \alpha x \left( rx^{r-1}\ln x + x^{r-1} \right) + \beta (x^r \ln x) \\ &= \left( r^2 + (\alpha - 1)r + \beta \right) x^r \ln x + \left( 2r - 1 + \alpha \right) x^r \end{split}\]

Therefore \(x^{r_1} \ln x\) is indeed a solution. Finally, the general solution of (1.41) when \(\Delta = 0\) is

(1.58)#\[ y(x)=(c_1 + c_2\ln x)x^{r_1} \ , \ \ c_1,c_2 \in \mathbb{R} \]

1.3.4. Case III: Complex Conjugate Roots#

If \(\Delta < 0\) then then \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) complex conjugate roots. In particular, write \(r_1 = \lambda + i \mu\) and \(r_2 = \lambda - i \mu\) where

(1.59)#\[ \lambda = \frac{1 - \alpha}{2} \hspace{20mm} \mu = \frac{ \sqrt{4 \beta - (\alpha - 1)^2}}{2} \]

The general solution of (1.41) when \(\Delta < 0\) is given by

(1.60)#\[\begin{split} y(x) &= c_1 x^{(\lambda + i\mu)} + c_2x^{(\lambda - i\mu)} \\ &= c_1 e^{(\lambda + i\mu) \ln x} + c_2 e^{(\lambda - i\mu) \ln x} \\ &= x^{\lambda} \left( c_1 e^{i \mu \ln x} + c_2 e^{-i\mu\ln x} \right) \end{split}\]

and fincally

(1.61)#\[ y(x) = A_1 x^\lambda \cos(\mu \ln x) + A_2 x^{\lambda} \sin(\mu \ln x) \ \ , \ \ \ A_1,A_2 \in \mathbb{R} \]

If we consider the range \(x < 0\) then we replace \(x\) in the general solution (1.61) by \(|x|\).

Apply the Wronskian test to show that the two solutions in (1.61) are linearly independent. The Wronskian is

(1.62)#\[\begin{split} w(y_1,y_2) = \left| \begin{array}{cc} y_1 &y_2 \\ y_1' & y_2' \end{array} \right| = y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2 \end{split}\]

and the functions \(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are linearly independent if \(w(y_1,y_2) \not = 0\). Let \(y_1 = x^{\lambda} \cos (\mu\ln x)\) and \(y_2 = x^{\lambda} \sin (\mu\ln x)\) and compute

(1.63)#\[\begin{split} w(y_1,y_2) &= x^{\lambda} \cos(\mu \ln x) \left( x^{\lambda} \ln x \sin(\mu\ln x) + x^{\lambda - 1} \cos(\mu \ln x) \mu \right) \\ & \quad \quad - \ x^{\lambda} \sin(\mu \ln x) \left( x^{\lambda} \ln x \cos(\mu \ln x) - x^{\lambda - 1} \sin(\mu \ln x) \mu \right) \\ &= \mu x^{2\lambda - 1} \end{split}\]

Therefore we conclude the solutions are linearly independent.

1.3.5. Examples#

Example 1. Find the unique solution of the equation

(1.64)#\[ x^2 y'' - xy' - 2y = 0 \ , \ \ y(1) = 0 \ , \ \ y'(1) = 1 \]

The characteristic polynomial is \(r^2 - 2r - 2 = 0\) and the roots are

(1.65)#\[ r_1 = 1 + \sqrt{3} \hspace{20mm} r_2 = 1 - \sqrt{3} \]

therefore the generral solution of (1.64) is

(1.66)#\[ y(x) = c_1 x^{1 + \sqrt{3}} + c_2 x^{1 - \sqrt{3}} \]

The initial condition \(y(1)=0\) implies \(y(1) = c_1 + c_2 = 0\) therfore \(c_2 = -c_1\)

(1.67)#\[ y(x) = c_1 \left( x^{1 + \sqrt{3}} - x^{1 - \sqrt{3}} \right) \]

Compute the derivative

(1.68)#\[ y'(x) = c_1 \left( \left( 1 + \sqrt{3} \right) x^{\sqrt{3}} - \left( 1 - \sqrt{3} \right) x^{-\sqrt{3}} \right) \]

and the condition \(y'(1) = 1\) yields \(c_1 = 1/(2 \sqrt{3})\). The unique solution of (1.64) is given by

(1.69)#\[ y(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{3}} \left(x^{1 + \sqrt{3}} - x^{1 - \sqrt{3}} \right) \]

Example 2. Find the unique solution of the equation

(1.70)#\[ x^2 y'' - 3xy' + 4y = 0 \ , \ \ y(1) = 1 \ , \ \ y'(1) = 0 \]

The characteristic polynomial is \(r^2 - 4r + 4 = 0\) and the roots are repeated \(r_1 = r_2 = 2\) therefore the general solution is

(1.71)#\[ y(x) = c_1 x^2 + c_2 x^2 \ln x \]

The initial condition \(y(1) = 1\) implies \(c_1 = 1\). Compute the derivative

(1.72)#\[ y'(x) = 2x + c_2 \left( 2x \ln x + x \right) \]

and the condition \(y'(1) = 0\) yields \(c_2 = -2\). The unique solution of (1.70) is given by

(1.73)#\[ y(x) = x^2 \left( 1 - 2 \ln x \right) \]
